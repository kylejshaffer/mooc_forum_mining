Instructions

A massive open online course (MOOC) is an online course that is open for anyone to enroll and participate. MOOCs are typically free of cost and are taken by thousands of students. Students progress through a MOOC by watching pre-recorded lecture videos, completing assignments, taking exams, and interacting with each other and the course staff using an online discussion forum.

Your goal in this HIT is to annotate a post from a MOOC discussion thread based on the speech acts that are present in the post. If you treat a MOOC discussion thread as a "conversation", the speech acts associated with a post indicate its function or purpose within the greater conversation.

We are interested in six speech act categories, defined below. It is possible for a post to be associated with more than one speech act or none.

Read the following speech act definitions carefully. For each speech act, we provide its definition, tips for recognizing the speech act, and examples. The "false" examples are posts that might be confused with the particular speech act, but should be labeled differently.

When you label a post, ask yourself: What is the purpose of the post within the context of the ongoing conversation? What is the author trying to achieve? You may need to review the context of the post (the posts leading-up to it) to understand the function of the post in the ongoing conversation. Then, select all of the speech act categories that apply.

If none of the speech acts apply, or if the post is not in English, select "Non-English or N/A".

We will also ask you to explain your choice(s).

You will be labeling posts written by real students and course staff from this MOOC.


QUESTION: The post contains a request for information about something related to the course material.

Additional Tips:
1. Questions can be expressed indirectly in the form of a statement: "I need help understanding question 3 in the homework."

2. Questions are typically answered by a clarification, elaboation, recommendation, or a yes/no response.

3. If the post contains a request for action from the instructor, then it should be labeled as an issue rather than a question.

4. From an instructor's perspective, questions are not necessarily bad. Asking questions and obtaining answers is part of a student's learning experience.

Examples:
True Question: "In Question 8 on the assignment I'm confused about code formatting. In lectures, the instructor said syntax should follow the form ________, but do you have to include _________? Any ideas what I'm doing wrong?"

False Question: "I'm barely keeping up with class lectures. Am I the only one who feels like the professor is going way too fast?"

This should be labeled as an issue because it describes a student's disatissfaction with the pace of the course and doesn't request additional information about course material. Implicitely, the student is asking the instructor to take corrective action (i.e., slow down the pace of the course).
ANSWER: The post is a direct response to a previously asked question and contains information that is likely to help answer the question.

Additional Tips:
1. Answers typically contain a clarification, elaboration, recommendation, or yes/no response.

2. While an answer may not definitively resolve a question, it should be useful in some way.

3. General feedback about a previously asked question (Example: "I had the same question.") should be labeled as a positive-acknowledgement instead of an answer

Examples:
True Answer: "The answer here should be of the form _________, applying the concepts we've covered so far this week. Hopefully that helps."

False Answer: "Our team has realized this is a problem when trying to submit assignments and we've notified the appropriate people at Coursera."

This should be labeled as an issue resolution since it pertains mostly to the course logistics, including submitting assignments, and not directly to course material.
ISSUE: The post expresses dissatisfaction with something related to the course and may require the course staff to take corrective action.

Additional Tips:
1. An issue must convey dissatisfaction with something related to the course.

2. An issue may require the instructor(s) or course staff to take corrective action.

3. An issue is not a request for information, but a request for action or awareness.

4. From an instructor's perspective, issues are a bad thing. They are not part of the learning experience. They convey dissatisfaction with the course content or course management

Examples:
True Issue: "The wording for question 6 was confusing. It was ambiguous in two ways. Please consider revising the wording, or giving students the points for this question."

False Issue: "I don't understand the distinction between concept X and concept Y. The professor made it seem like these were interchangeable, but now in the homework they look like different things. Any help is appreciated."
This should be labeled as a question since it is primarily conveying confusion on the part of the student and asking for help in the last sentence.
ISSUE RESOLUTION: The post is a direct response to a previously raised issue and attempts to resolve it.

Additional Tips:
1. An Issue Resolution must be in response to an issue and not a question.

2. An Issue Resolution may simply indicate that the instructor or course staff is aware of the issue. It may not necessarily indicate that the issue has been (or will be) resolved.

Examples:
True Issue Resolution: "We are aware of a glitch in the submission form for Homework 2. As a result, the last question has been awarded to each student as a free point."

False Issue Resolution: "I can see where you might think that. However, I think the instructor was trying to say ________ in the lecture."
This should be labeled as an answer since it attempts to provide additional information directly related to course content, specifically about a lecture.
POSITIVE ACKNOWLEDGEMENT: The post conveys a positive sentiment about a previous post. Positive sentiments include agreement, encouragement, and support.

Additional Tips:
1. A positive acknowledgement may encourage a previous author, but not necessarily attempt to answer a question or resolve an issue.

2. A positive acknowledgement may not necessarily contain positive language. For example: "I don't get it either. I found this confusing too."

3. Be sure that the post in question is responding to a previous author's post. General positive posts about course content without responding to a previous post author should not be considered positive acknowledgement.

Examples:
True Positive Acknowledgement: "I'm glad I'm not the only one stuck on this! This was definitely confusing for me too!"

False Positive Acknowledgement: "I don't understand the previous student's problem. I figure since this is a free course, we should be happy with that instead of complaining."
This should be labeled a negative acknowledgement. Although the student is defending the course in a positive way, the primary reference for this post is in opposition to a previous post.
NEGATIVE ACKNOWLEDGEMENT: The post conveys a negative sentiment about a previous post. Negative sentiments include disagreement, discouragement, confusion, and frustration.

Additional tips:
1. Negative acknowledgements reference a previous student's post. If the negative sentiment is about the course content, then it is an issue.

2. Negative acknowledgments may urge an author to look at the course in a different way, indicating that their current attitude is unproductive or inappropriate.

3. Be sure that the post in question is responding to a previous author's post. General negative posts about course content should not be considered negative acknowledgement.

Examples:
True Negative Acknowledgement: "Question 4 may have been difficult, but part of learning new material is working at it. No sense in complaining."

False Negative Acknowledgement #1: "I have to say I'm frustrated that the homework hasn't been posted yet. It puts unnecessary pressure on students to release homework so close to the due date."
This should be labeled as an issue since it primarily references course logistics and not another student's post.

False Negative Acknowledgement #2: "Agreed. It was frustrating not getting the first two questions right and having to submit them over and over again."
This should be labeled as a positive acknowledgement. Although the post is expressing frustration, it is primarily agreeing with a previous post.
